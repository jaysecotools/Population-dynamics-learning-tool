Introduction to Population Dynamics

Population dynamics is the study of how and why populations change over time. In Tasmania's unique ecosystems, understanding these changes is crucial for effective conservation and ecosystem management.

As future conservation professionals, you'll need to understand:

  • How populations grow and decline
  • Factors affecting population size
  • The impact of human activities on ecosystems
  • Management strategies for threatened species

Tasmania's isolation has created unique population dynamics with many endemic species (found nowhere else in the world) that require special conservation attention.

Why This Matters for Your Work

In your Certificate III studies and future conservation work, you'll:

  • Monitor wildlife populations
  • Assist with habitat restoration projects
  • Participate in threatened species recovery programs
  • Help manage invasive species
  • Contribute to sustainable land management practices

Understanding population dynamics will help you make informed decisions in all these areas.

Key Concepts in Population Dynamics

Population Growth Models

Populations can grow in different ways:

Exponential Growth

When resources are unlimited, populations can grow exponentially (J-shaped curve). This is rare in nature but may occur when a species colonizes a new area with no competition.

Logistic Growth

More common in nature, where growth slows as the population reaches the environment's carrying capacity (S-shaped curve). This is what we typically see in Tasmanian ecosystems.

Factors Affecting Population Size

Population sizes change due to four main factors:

  • Birth rate (natality) - Number of offspring produced
  • Death rate (mortality) - Number of individuals dying
  • Immigration - Individuals moving into the population
  • Emigration - Individuals moving out of the population

In Tasmania, these factors are influenced by:

  • Climate and weather patterns
  • Availability of food and shelter
  • Disease outbreaks
  • Predator-prey relationships
  • Human activities (land use, pollution, conservation efforts)

Carrying Capacity

The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely. In Tasmania, carrying capacities can change due to:

  • Habitat loss or restoration
  • Climate change impacts
  • Introduction of invasive species
  • Changes in land management practices

Tasmanian Species Case Examples

Endemic and Iconic Species

Tasmanian Devil (Sarcophilus harrisii)

Population Status: Endangered due to Devil Facial Tumor Disease (DFTD)

Conservation Efforts: Captive breeding programs, disease-free populations on Maria Island and Tasman Peninsula

Thylacine (Thylacinus cynocephalus)

Population Status: Extinct (last known individual died in 1936)

Historical Factors: Hunting, habitat loss, disease, and competition with introduced species led to decline

Eastern Quoll (Dasyurus viverrinus)

Population Status: Extinct on mainland Australia, vulnerable in Tasmania

Threats: Predation by feral cats, habitat loss, vehicle collisions

Wedge-tailed Eagle (Aquila audax)

Population Status: Secure but threatened by habitat loss and human persecution

Conservation: Protected species, nest site protection programs

Introduced Species Challenges

Introduced species can dramatically alter population dynamics of native species:

European Rabbit (Oryctolagus cuniculus)

Introduced in the 19th century, rabbits compete with native herbivores and cause significant land degradation. Their population dynamics show boom-bust cycles influenced by:

  • Food availability
  • Disease (myxomatosis, rabbit calicivirus)
  • Predation
  • Control measures

Feral Cats (Felis catus)

Predate on native small mammals, birds, and reptiles. Their populations are difficult to control due to:

  • High reproductive rates
  • Generalist diet
  • Ability to thrive in various habitats

Tasmanian Case Studies

Devil Facial Tumor Disease (DFTD)

A contagious cancer that has caused dramatic population declines in Tasmanian devils since the 1990s.

Population Impacts:

  • Local population declines of up to 90% in some areas
  • Changes in age structure (fewer adults surviving)
  • Potential ecological cascades (changes in prey populations)

Management Responses:

  • Establishment of disease-free insurance populations
  • Research into genetic resistance and vaccines
  • Monitoring of wild populations

Tasmanian Wedge-tailed Eagle Conservation

Management of this apex predator demonstrates complex population dynamics.

Challenges:

  • Low reproductive rate (one chick every 2 years typically)
  • Territorial behavior limits population density
  • Threats from habitat loss and human persecution

Conservation Strategies:

  • Protection of nest sites from disturbance
  • Working with landowners to reduce persecution
  • Monitoring of breeding success

Macquarie Island Ecosystem Restoration

A successful example of population management through eradication of invasive species.

Issues:

  • Introduced rabbits, rats, and mice were devastating native vegetation and seabird populations
  • Complex predator-prey dynamics made control difficult

Solution:

  • World's largest eradication program (2010-2014)
  • Removal of all rabbits, rats, and mice
  • Resulting in dramatic recovery of native vegetation and seabird populations

Quick Facts

  • Tasmania has over 1,800 known endemic species
  • About 40% of Tasmania is protected in reserves and World Heritage Areas
  • The Tasmanian devil is the world's largest surviving carnivorous marsupial
  • Wedge-tailed eagles have a wingspan up to 2.3 meters
  • Eastern quolls were declared extinct on mainland Australia in 1963

Key Terms

Population
A group of individuals of the same species living in a particular area
Carrying Capacity (K)
The maximum population size an environment can sustain
Endemic Species
Species native to and found only in a specific area
Exponential Growth
Population growth without resource limitations
Logistic Growth
Population growth that slows as it approaches carrying capacity
